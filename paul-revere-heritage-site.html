A Canton Case Study

Forty acres. Ten years.
Transformed.

How Canton turned 40 acres of contaminated industrial wasteland — abandoned in a 2006 bankruptcy, a half-mile from downtown — into a thriving public park, two restored Paul Revere–era buildings, and a 300-plus-unit neighborhood. A case study in patient public-private collaboration, and in the kind of long-horizon civic work Lisa Lopez has been part of for more than a decade.

The Plymouth Rubber Company site, abandoned 2006. Forty acres. A half-mile from downtown Canton.

This was once one of the most consequential industrial sites in early American history. By 2006 it was forty acres of contaminated wasteland.

Today, the same forty acres are home to a public park, more than 300 homes, two restored Revere-era buildings, and seven acres of open space preserved in perpetuity. Getting from one to the other took ten years.

The same forty acres, today.
The Site Then

In 1801, at age 65, Paul Revere risked his entire fortune to build a copper rolling mill on this stretch of land along the East Branch of the Neponset River — the first copper rolling mill in America. Within two years his copper was sheathing the dome of the Massachusetts State House and the hull of the USS Constitution. He moved his bell foundry to Canton in 1804, and one of those bells still hangs in Canton's First Parish Unitarian Universalist Church today, installed in 1824 and ringing for two hundred years.

A century after Revere, the Plymouth Rubber Company took over the site and ran it through most of the 1900s. By 2006, Plymouth Rubber was bankrupt. The factories were abandoned. The land was contaminated. Two Revere-era structures — the 1850 Copper Rolling Mill and the 1845 Draft Horse Barn — still stood among the ruin, but they were unreachable, unused, and falling apart.

This was the situation Canton inherited.

The Pivot — 2012

Almost any path forward required money the town didn't have. Environmental remediation alone would run into the millions; preserving open space, restoring two historic buildings, and shaping the development of 300-plus residential units would require many millions more.

In 2012, after two earlier failed attempts, Canton finally adopted the Community Preservation Act — a state program that lets municipalities raise dedicated funds for open space, historic preservation, and affordable housing. Lisa Lopez championed the third and successful campaign. Without the CPA in place, almost everything that followed would have lacked a funding mechanism.

The Plan — 2015

Behind the 2015 Town Meeting vote was years of public-private negotiation, led on the town side by Canton's Select Board and the newly formed Paul Revere Heritage Commission. The agreement that came before Town Meeting was layered and ambitious:

  • The town would purchase 7 acres of open space for a public park, with a conservation restriction in perpetuity.
  • Another 2.5 acres would preserve the two Revere-era buildings — the 1850 Copper Rolling Mill and the 1845 Draft Horse Barn — to be restored as a destination restaurant and a public museum.
  • The private developer, Canton Holdings LLC, would clean up the contaminated land and build 300-plus units of housing — rental and owner-occupied — with more than 100 units reserved for residents 55 and older.
  • A community dog park would be included.

At the time of the Town Meeting vote and the open space purchase, Lisa chaired Canton's Community Preservation Committee — the body responsible for allocating the CPA funds that made the town's portion of the deal possible.

The Work — 2015 to 2024

What followed was a decade of demolition, environmental cleanup, restoration, and construction. Contaminated soil was removed. The Canton River's diversion channel was reshaped. The Copper Rolling Mill and the Draft Horse Barn — both more than 170 years old, both falling apart — were rebuilt brick by brick.

Lisa stayed involved throughout. She chaired the Open Space Committee that designed the seven-acre public park. She supported development negotiations from Canton's Select Board, including securing the planned community dog park, and continued that work afterward. She is currently a member of the Paul Revere Advisory Council and a personal donor to the private fundraising effort.

Today

The 1850 Copper Rolling Mill now houses Northern Spy, a New England restaurant built around a wood-fired hearth. The 1845 Draft Horse Barn now houses the Paul Revere Museum of Discovery & Innovation — MoDI — with four floors of interactive exhibits about the Reveres' work and Canton's industrial history. The seven-acre public park, known today as the Revere Green, draws walkers, dog owners, families, and event-goers from across the region.

In May 2026, the Revere Green was dedicated to Victor Del Vecchio — Lisa's husband and a longtime member of Canton's Select Board and the Paul Revere Heritage Commission — in recognition of more than a decade of leadership on the project.

The 300-plus residential units have brought new neighbors within walking distance of downtown businesses, and the over-55 units have given longtime Canton residents a place to downsize without leaving town.

Environmental stewardship. Decades of industrial contamination cleaned up. Open space protected by conservation restriction in perpetuity. The Canton River's diversion channel reshaped to handle modern flood risk.
Transit-oriented housing. The site sits within walking distance of the commuter rail to Boston — a textbook example of dense residential development placed where it belongs: near jobs, near transit, reducing automobile dependence.
A downsizing path for seniors. More than 100 homes reserved for residents 55 and older have given longtime Canton families a place to downsize without leaving town — and added their former, larger homes back to the inventory available to younger families.
Local jobs. A decade of construction work, plus ongoing maintenance and restaurant operations on a site that, before this project, employed no one.
Economic development for downtown. New residents within walking distance of Canton Center support local businesses with foot traffic and customer base they didn't have before.
Public history made vibrant. Two buildings from Paul Revere's Canton works — where in 1801 he founded the American copper industry — have been restored to community use. One houses Northern Spy, a destination restaurant. The other houses MoDI, a four-floor museum that draws visitors from across the region. Canton's history is now a place you can walk through.

Lisa Lopez has been part of this work for more than a decade — championing the funding mechanism that made the public side possible, chairing the committee that designed the seven-acre Revere Green, supporting development negotiations from the Select Board, and continuing on the Advisory Council today.

It's the kind of long-horizon, complicated, public-private work she'd bring to Beacon Hill — for Canton, Stoughton, and Avon.
